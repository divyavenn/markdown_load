how to become exceptional

frameworks for achievement and despair

You don't like where you are.

It seems like some people get where they want to be effortlessly; they rise ever upwards, guided by an albatross that passed you by. Meanwhile, you’re stuck waiting for something: clarity, luck, or permission.

I’m happy to say several of my friends are like this. Not only are they highly accomplished in different domains, they’re unusually resilient, thoughtful, easygoing, and self-aware. Many conquered the odds and underwent a lot of hardship to get where they are.

Despite the diversity of personality and experience, certain common frameworks connect them all. Learning from this pattern helped me accelerate my own life significantly.

Waking up early, eating healthy, drinking water, working out….good advice overused to the point of cliché. The mental frameworks that set you up for achievement are far more subtle.

Stop waiting for clarity.

From afar it looks like successful people somehow magically know where to put their efforts. I asked careful questions, hoping to figure out the following:

How did you, years ago, know that this would work out? Why did you choose this as opposed to something else?

I knew I had potential. I wanted to figure out my own path forward. How had they found the clarity that continued to elude me?

They all had the same answer. They didn’t.

They had no idea! They invested a whole lot of consistent effort and often sacrificed a great deal for something they had zero reassurance would work. In their history they had done it time and time again and had sometimes failed. But nothing stopped them from trying once more.

I, like most of us, spent my life shivering on the edge of the pool. They leapt.

Internalize the J-curve.

I see this pattern everywhere: it takes around two years for any given endeavor to start reaping impressive fruit.

There will be long periods of time where it seems like a given project simply is not working. You’re not making any real money, you’re not gaining any recognition, you remain stubbornly on the lower end of the skill curve.

In those moments, it’s easy to get discouraged and give up. Most people do. Their initial excitement fades, and with no intermittent rewards to keep them going, their effort fizzles out.

Reminding yourself in those moments that you’re still on the lower end of the J-curve - focusing on the process rather than the outcome - is what allows you to continue.

When people say someone "did everything right," it usually means they acted with caution. Hedged their bets. But you can do everything "right" and still lose. People never remember this.

They lose, and question their methods. Resolve to play it even safer next time. They win, they think they're invincible.

Life's random. It's all a probability function. You create your own luck by being prolific.

Learn to believe what is useful, not what is true.

Virgil wrote “audentis fortuna adiuvat” - fortune favors the brave. Remaining brave becomes infinitely harder when you lend your ear to pessimists and defeatists.

Job market statistics, divorce rates, epidemics of obesity and mental illness, Reddit subs populated by thousands where any trace of optimism or self satisfaction is ridiculed…

If you look for it, there’s a lot of evidence that your chances of making it are slim to nonexistant. Disheartened, you second guess yourself. Perhaps it’s better to settle for whatever you can easily get, or what you already have.

The truth is this: there is no utility ruminating over what your chances “actually” are. Whatever they may be, they’ll be maximized by consistent effort, improvement, and resilience on your part. Getting easily discouraged is the Achilles heel of even the most brilliant and talented.

The answer is simple: don’t engage with naysayers. Simply and quietly turn away.

This requires a fair bit of ruthlessness sometimes. It feels cruel to distance yourself from good people simply because they make you feel worse about the world. It feels like sticking your head in the sand to ignore an entire worldview.

The problem is not merely that you feel bad. Often the points of view that upset us are the most important to listen to.

So what’s the line between utilitarianism and delusion?

Any worldview that motivates you to work, improve, and put yourself out there is utilitarian.

Reframe your relationship with failure.

I can tell you now for a fact that behind every remarkable success story is more failure than you would imagine - times in which no onlooker would bet a cent that this person would ever amount to anything.

Even the most gifted have times of despair. Until they gain a strong footing and establish their reputation with achievement, they flounder. Their talents go completely unrecognized, their prospects remain bleak.

Willingness to try is the greatest unspoken asset of the young. A few hard knocks from life and it usually vanishes. Remember that it is easy and seductive to find reasons not to go after what you want.

Reality follows no neat formula of probabilities. Luck is a fickle mistress - she can place a golden goose in your lap one day and wreak havoc on your best-laid plans the next.

There is no such thing as a happy ending, or a tragic one. There are only middles. The extraordinary see failure as a necessary part of every journey.

It took me a long time to understand the importance of forgiveness. Without you don't have resilience. I spent most of my life suffocated under the cumulative weight of all the mistakes I had made, and gave up on myself as a result. There truly is no greater tragedy.

Never run the Red Queen’s race.

"Well, in our country," said Alice, still panting a little, "you'd generally get to somewhere else—if you run very fast for a long time, as we've been doing."

"A slow sort of country!" said the Queen. "Now, here, you see, it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place. If you want to get somewhere else, you must run at least twice as fast as that!"

Headspace, time, and energy are provably finite resources that you have to fight to conserve. Distractions will clutter up your life rapidly if you let them.

Something unique to our era is the vast number of well-meaning people pushing you to unnecessarily overcomplicate your life.

Design your life to be as simple, streamlined, and convenient as possible so you can focus on what matters. Stressful relationships are productivity killers. Overly intensive personal care routines take precious headspace away from projects and learning. Disorganization of any kind is a constant drain.

Pouring energy into fruitless endeavors is like running the Red Queen’s race.

All the outliers I know have been accused at one point, by someone or the other, of being selfish with their attention. They simply refuse to engage with things (or people) that are unnecessarily frustrating or draining.

You have to aggressively, constantly, intentionally simplify. Ruthlessly paring your life down to its most impactful components is an incredibly important mental framework.

The truth is, you can only really focus on one thing. The very act of deciding what to focus on can occupy more energy than you think. When life itself is a balancing act, there’s precious little energy left to move forward.

Consistently direct your thoughts towards execution. Your priority is simply the most impactful thing you can do right now. The earlier in the day you tackle it, the more progress you will make.

Simplicity is next to godliness. Easy to get overwhelmed by all there is to do. The distant future is irrelevant, as are regrets you didn’t start sooner.

Give and receive help easily.

The greatest silent privilege in the world is social capital.

A good network is an immensely powerful asset, but it goes so much deeper than that. Humans are experiential learners; we often don't internalize that something is possible until we see someone in our vicinity do it.

Everyone you meet knows something or someone you don’t. Dismissing someone because they aren’t successful or important enough is how you end up devoting a lot of unnecessary energy to status games.

People are eager to return the favor when someone goes even a little out of their way to be helpful. You can do a lot of good in the world by making a timely introduction, being a bouncing board for ideas, or even just sharing relevant experiences.

Thoughts like “What am I getting out of this?” are unproductive. Fostering goodwill pays big dividends in ways you would never expect.

Don’t be reluctant to accept favors. People crave closeness and connection; reciprocity is what cements relationships and it has to start somewhere. Allow people to do things for you. Take the liberty of asking.

Make it a principle to help whenever possible and valuable opportunities will materialize organically and joyously.

Everything is a skill, not a feeling.

You have to not to take your own shortcomings personally. We all struggle with something; it’s a part and parcel of being human.

That rare balance of clarity and empathy (both for others and yourself) comes when you stop seeing people as inherently deserving or not deserving.

I blow up but only because I’m justifiably angry → I need to be better at governing my temper

People don’t like me because they’re prejudiced → Something I do is off-putting. If I figure out what it is, I could stop.

She lashes out but it’s only because she's insecure → She doesn't have a handle on her emotions.

This framework allows you to acknowledge and navigate poor behavior (others’s and your own) without lapsing into criticism and judgement. No one is to be hated, but some people do need to be avoided.

Extraordinary people aren't free from bad impulses and negative feelings, but they know how to think past their own self-sabotaging minds.

Giving into every whim in the name of “self care” is how you end up dissatisfied, self-absorbed, and tyrannical.

Being incredibly harsh and critical of your own mind's workings is how you end up paralyzed by anxiety, neuroticism, and self loathing.

Acceptance and discipline can and must coexist.

The exceptional do not fall into spirals of bitterness, but nor do they brush off the consequences of wrongdoing. They fall, sigh, shrug, dust themselves off, correct course, and keep running.

This how you maintain both peace of mind and empathy.

Don’t sit on ideas.

Saying you’re going to accomplish something gives you a temporary dopamine hit without the work - that’s why discussing your plans is so gratifying. Like all forms of cheap fulfillment, do your best not to indulge it.

Ruminating endlessly on the future does you no favors either. Some people spend their whole lives crippled by anxiety and indecision - life only truly begins once you just pick a thing and do it.

Even if it turns out to be a mistake, you can repivot at any time while having learned a lot on the way. There’s nothing to be gained by postponing execution.

Last but not least, nothing is beneath you.

Chores, blue collar work, min wage jobs, internships at unknown firms, learning something from scratch...

Everyone has to start somewhere. Many of my friends demonstrated their competence early on by doing menial work exceptionally well (they were broke and needed the money, but this put them well ahead of people who had the luxury of loafing off).

The only valid reason not to do something is that there's a genuinely more productive use of your time.

Minimum wage work vs apprenticeship where you learn a lot? Answer is clear. Zero reason to feel proud as you sit playing video games, thinking you're too smart to flip burgers.

The people I learned these lessons from are some of the most cherished in my life. Meeting them brought me to the deeply painful realization that I had squandered my potential.

For most of my life I expected very little of myself. My upbringing was chaotic, isolated, and without guidance. A stable job, good personal habits, and a peaceful home life felt barely within reach.

In truth, I was the only one holding myself back. Anyone who realizes this will, beyond a shadow of a doubt, emerge triumphant.

As I reframed my thinking, my destiny changed before my eyes. I learned to see the world as my oyster….passion, adventure, and achievement as inevitable.

Gently, consistently redirect the mind away from unproductive thoughts. All intentional growth depends on this skill.

I can already see my efforts bearing fruit.
