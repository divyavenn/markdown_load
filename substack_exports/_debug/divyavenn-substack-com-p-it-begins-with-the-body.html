it begins with the body

lessons in happiness from the bathhouse

The temple of the spirit is the body, not the mind.

Oh, the human mind can be a beautiful thing, but that’s as rare as grace. The happiest people only use their minds when they have to. As a general rule, the more you live in your head, the unhappier you are.

I know America never figured this out because we don’t have bathhouse culture.

My recent trip to East Asia was a godforsaken carousel of recycled air, fluorescent lightning, cramped seating, and lines. I was nauseous. My tongue was thick and furred. Errant noises made me flinch, and nothing felt real or good.

When I finally oozed into my hotel room in Osaka, I wanted to lie down much more than I wanted a bath, but there’s a specific griminess to long haul flights that feels like it’ll contaminate a bed.

Japan is known for its bathhouses. Famous onsens are radioactively heated, teeming with natural mineral waters, bejeweled with fallen citrus fruit. The hotel’s onsen was just a large, shallow pool on the top floor, softly steaming beside a line of showers. Still, it was transcendental.

Sitting down, the hot water just came up to my chin. The heat seeped into my skin like a blessing, untangling what felt like endless frayed knots in my nerves. My entire attention was on how my toes felt clenching and flexing in the steaming water. In that midnight bath I lost myself for hours in my own body, experimenting with the flexion of every muscle, reacquainting myself with my own breath.

I was not bored. I was not anxious. I was not lonely. I had no desire to look at my phone. I was fully inhabiting myself in a way I hadn’t for weeks.

Afterwards I was unbelievably, unscientifically refreshed and awake, even though I hadn’t slept in twenty-four hours. I watched the sunrise breakfasting on miso soup, rice, natto, and fermented vegetables followed by a great day exploring Osaka.

the body in culture

Wellness in America often feels like a battle against the body rather than an invitation into it. People treat their bodies like a beast to be conquered: punished with spin classes, regulated with calorie math, carefully dosed with supplements and substances.

There’s a subtle disregard for and alienation from our fundamental bodily perceptions, like our body is just machinery accompanying the all-important brain. We don’t have rituals of quietude or communal restoration. There is no appreciation or intentionality around the art of simply being.

In ancient Japan, bathhouses were often attached to temples. Cleansing the body was inseparable from cleansing the spirit. It is said that villagers watched a wounded egret heal itself by visiting the hot springs of Dōgo and so first discovered the healing powers of the onsen.

In Finland, too, the sauna has spiritual significance. Traditionally it is where babies are born and where the dying are washed one last time. They say even today that sauna is the poor man’s pharmacy: “If tar, spirits, or sauna won’t cure you, the disease is probably fatal.”

In Rome thethermae was for more than public hygiene. The Stoics believed in harmony between body and mind: bathing was part of that moral regimen. To maintain your composure in the forum, you first had to sweat in the caldarium.

We’re used to thinking of happiness as a mindset. But across cultures and centuries, it was understood more like a posture.

This publication is called observing happiness because for the longest time all I wanted was to just be happy and I didn’t know how. No one around me had especially bad lives, but clearly none of them were really happy except in rare, shining moments.

There is more wisdom in your body than in your deepest philosophy.

— Friedrich Nietzsche

A psychologist might call my natural temperament manic-depressive. My brain chemistry let met get excited and delighted very easily, but the intense highs came with intense lows and (worse) periods of gray nothingness. The rollercoaster was fun, but I had no resilience or ability to self-regulate.

Lasting contentment always felt incomprehensible. I read neuroscience books, philosophy texts, everything eloquent and sensible people had to say about it. I guess you could say it was my special interest.

I unlocked consistent, natural, effervescent happiness through the body, not the mind. The mind is a brilliant tool, but it’s often misused—overworked, under-trained, or treated as the sole site of selfhood.

I’ve realized the mind can impede happiness very well, but it can’t create it. Euphoria? Passion? Obsession? Satisfaction? Yes. But not the kind of happiness I’m talking about: simple and sustainable, a soul-deep wellspring of contentment that makes everything from friendship to work to health easier and sweeter.

It’s even how I got over my social anxiety.

(I go into how to keep your mind from making you miserable in a another essay, called happiness from the inside out).

the body in popularity

Most people don’t realize that social anxiety, overthinking, even chronic insecurity are all rooted in bracing: holding something in, holding something back, straining towards something that’s just out of reach.

I was talking to a friend about this. He told me he used to talk with this strained, overly bright, vaguely apologetic voice—a kind of “customer service cheerfulness” he wasn’t even aware he was performing.

Then he started taking singing lessons.

He learned to feel where his voice sat in his body. To consciously relax the different muscles in his throat and chest. For the first time he became aware of the constant tension in his speaking voice and deliberately dissolved it.

There was an immediate difference. Not just in how nice he sounded, but in how he moved through the world. Conversations flowed more naturally. He approached interactions with more openness and felt that energy magnified and returned. In physical, literal way, he’d stopped bracing against connection.

I’d had the exact same experience and I’d never had the chance to talk about it with anyone before him. People ask me often how I overcome social anxiety - a really, really big component of it was changing how I used my body. Learning to be relaxed and easy in my movements, slow and deliberate, keep my voice unstrained and friendly and open.

Lao Tzu famously said, the best are like water. He didn’t mean physically, but I think it’s true in that sensne as well.

I’ve noticed anger, fear, discomfort, insecurity, annoyance, and frustration all feel similar in the body: they feel…rigid, like ice. But I can be as simple and fluid and inevitable as water. When I feel myself tense up for any reason, I exhale and melt.

I remember, like a prayer, how water moves over rocks: never forced, always arriving. The emotion or thought that bothered me miraculously loses its hold.

“The highest goodness is like water. Water benefits all things and does not compete.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching

Tension is the ultimate enemy of belonging. We intuitively trust people who are calm in their bodies. Not performatively calm, not the flat affect of the artificially composed, but people who are genuinely regulated, relaxed, grounded.

They’re easier to talk to, easier to believe, easier to love. They seem at perpetually at ease in the world and give you permission to be at ease too. Their confidence is the art of surrendering gracefully to the unfamiliar.

How you feel changes how you make others feel, and your feelings are somatic states. We’re all constantly picking up on each other’s nervous system cues.

When people say “just be yourself!” or “be authentic!” what they really mean is be relaxed. Posture, breath, and voice all have massive power in shaping how we’re received.

the body in neuroscience

Modern neuroscience is catching up to what many cultures have intuited: that self-regulation, or lack of it, starts in the body and is merely amplified by the brain.

As Bessel van der Kolk explains in The Body Keeps the Score, physical dysregulation reduces activity in the neocortex. Your rational mind, aware of logic, time, and context, goes offline. Older systems, more primal systems take over—the amygdala, the insula, the vagus nerve. You become your storied past and all your neuroses.

Similarly, neuroscientist Stephen Porges pioneered polyvagal theory, reframing calm, openness, and even social presence as somatic states instead of mental ones.

When we’re physically relaxed, our ventral vagus nerve activates, giving us the capacity for playfulness, curiosity, humor, liveliness, and connection. It’s a state of relaxation, safety, and social engagement.

When that system shuts down we enter survival mode, even if we know intellectually that it’s counterproductive. Almost always, the people around sense the shift and clam up in turn.

You can’t think your way out of a nervous system in distress. It’s why advice like “just be confident” feels almost like mockery. The harder you try to contort your mind into a particular shape, the worse the results.

So what’s a nervous wreck to do?

the body in joy

The happiest people aren’t the most intelligent or accomplished or even the most self aware. What they are is unusually embodied — which is to say they are constantly, quietly aware of their body, highly attuned to it, at ease in it, in control of it.

They move with ease. They smile and laugh readily. They touch with intention, stretch decadently, breathe and eat slowly. They inhabit each moment without getting caught up in a narration of it. They know that mindfulness is triggered by a cognitive act but arrives as a somatic state.

They dance.

Dance, singing, martial arts, yoga, and meditation are valuable and real forms of therapy. I do all of them as often as I can. Progressing in each requires increasing awareness and control over your muscles, learning to pay attention to their cues, finding safety and joy within your own skin. There was even a meta-analysis in Complementary Therapies in Clinical Practice that found that dancing — just dancing — can alleviate clinic depression and anxiety as well as pharmaceuticals.

People often object to dancing because they feel awkward. They assume some people are just naturally good at it and some never will be. As someone who used to be insufferably awkward at dancing, anyone can learn to dance easily and gracefully. You’ll find, as I did, that as you learn how to relax into yourself and move naturally, many other things will unexpectedly start coming more naturally too.

Emotional regulation, focus, pleasure, awe: all these beneficent states of mind are equally states of the body. You cannot be joyful if you’re chronically tensed. You cannot be grateful if you’re holding your breath.

The older I grow, the more I think embodiment is the most fundamental skill of happiness. It plays a crucial role in emotional regulation, in social belonging, in being a good lover, in health and wellbeing, in everything that makes a life worthy and joyous.

“You don’t think your way into a new kind of living. You live your way into a new kind of thinking.”

— Alan Watts
