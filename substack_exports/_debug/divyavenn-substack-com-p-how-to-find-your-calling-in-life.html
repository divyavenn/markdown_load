how to find your calling in life

the only reliable path to contentment

A lot of people feel scammed.

They were told you should find something you’re passionate about, and that would make you happy. Don’t be like people who chase money! They end up soulless and depressed and superficial. This often means taking impractical but enjoyable coursework in university and working a job that isn’t actually all that interesting after all and also doesn’t pay well.

A lot of people also feel completely lacking in purpose.

They were told money is important, it gives you freedom. Work hard early and then you get to relax and enjoy the fruits of your labor. Don’t be like people who chase passion! They’re naive shmucks and also broke.

I’m a software engineer, so most people around me are the second type. They don’t worry about money, sure, but they end up chasing highs elsewhere — relationships that are stimulating rather than loving, consumerist hobbies, dining, travel, always chasing the Next Great purchase or experience.

These things are not inherently bad except when they’re used as anesthetics. Distraction-as-purpose.

So how do you avoid the Scylla and Charybdis of passion vs materialism?

There is a third option. And no, this essay is not about ikagai.

I know very few people who actually derive a sense of real purpose from their work. They are, without exception, the happiest people I know.

Interestingly enough, they would not call themselves passionate people. Their days are methodical, focused, and emotionally even. What they are is obsessed.

Passionate people give up all the time. They get bored. They take breaks. They pivot. Passion is a phase. Obsession is a way of life. Passion is charming. Obsession is boring. Passion says, "I love doing this." Obsession says, "I need to get better or there is no point in living.”

This isn’t moral judgment, it’s just the nature of passion. No high can last forever.

Obsession is not inherently virtuous. In fact, it often makes you neglect your relationships and end up insufferable to work with. S once told me that multiple people have accused him of being he’s selfish with his time. He admits this is fair. In the past he’s chosen building cool things over money, health, family, and even love.

Despite all the tradeoffs, he’s a ridiculously contented and happy go lucky person, because obsession simplifies your life in a way that makes happiness easy. You have just one question on your mind at all times: how do I get better?

There’s a really beautiful movie called Jiro Dreams of Sushi. I found it from an unlikely tweet: a successful trader called it “the best trading manual ever made. all the trading books and guides are bullshit. just watch jiro dreams of sushi.”

This documentary about Jirō Ono is an intimate portrait of greatness. The world’s most exacting food critics concur: Jirō makes sushi so good you wouldn’t imagine it was possible. When I first heard this, I was skeptical: how good can you get at slapping together rice and fish?

Jirō uses the same ingredients and tools as everyone else, but his technique has been honed for seventy years. He continually samples every fish delivery and tells his apprentices how to adjust the preparation for that particular specimen. This tuna is dense because it’s on the younger side; marinate it for so many minutes longer.

At one point he talks about one of his innovations: massaging octopus for a full forty-five minutes — no more, no less — to best bring out its flavor. Imagine how much you have to think about and iterate preparing octopus to know that with perfect confidence!

Now imagine how many similar experiments he carried out that yielded no such revelations. You have to go down thousands of useless rabbit holes for the few epiphanies that set you apart as a true master. If you’re attached to being great as vanity project, you’d get discouraged long before you get there. The process itself has to be what fulfills you.

One critic laid out what makes a truly great chef:

  1. Take your work seriously. Hold yourself to a ridiculous standard.

  2. Always strive to improve.

  3. Be clean and methodical.

  4. Be impatient. A poor collaborator. Stubborn as hell.

  5. You need to care enormously about what everything related to your craft

This is the shokunin spirit. The Japanese word shokunin loosely translates to "craftsman," but more than that it’s a way of being: monastic, sacred, and lonely.

Jirō was kicked out at nine. His alcoholic father slurred at him, “Work hard because there is no home for you here anymore.” There was no backup plan or guidance or advice. There was no, “If it doesn’t work out, you can always come home.”

Jirō says when you tell children that, they grow up to be failures. He toiled in restaurants to survive and found a religion in it.

His son, Yoshikazu, prepares sushi alongside him. He has been working at the restaurant his whole life. He is over 50 and still called an apprentice. One day he will inherit the restaurant. For decades he was not permitted to make the omelet.

Jirō dislikes holidays and will only close down his restaurant for national holidays. The filmmakers did manage to capture one of his rare visits to his hometown. He and Yoshikazu paid obeisance to the graves of Jirō’s parents. Jirō said, with an only faintly bitter laugh, “I don’t even know why I come here. They never cared for me.”

He resents his parents for the difficulties of his early life and yet credits those difficulties for the heights he reached. I remember discussing this moment with K — that Jirō is probably right, and yet many (most) children who were thrown out like him probably lived paycheck to paycheck their whole lives or even just died in the gutter.

Everything about the documentary points to Jirō being a naturally obsessive person. I think he would have ended up great whether his parents kicked him out or not. Maybe it would have taken a few more years, maybe it would have been soba or calligraphy rather than sushi, but he would have gotten there.

The thing about needing to work to survive, though, is that it keeps you safe from the trap many luckier young people fall into: jumping from thing to thing because they lose interest.

It’s simply not enough for work to be interesting. Interesting wears off. You need work that pulls you in like gravity. Early hardship forces you to stick to something long enough to pave the way for the kind of obsession that guarantees lifelong peace and self-assurance.

Also, having the luxury to pick and choose, they often mistakenly chase the wrong thing. Freedom, for example, or happiness. The whole point of obsession is that it makes your life smaller in the best possible way. It quiets the mind. It frees you from many desires. It also doesn’t make you happy, exactly, as least not in the way people imagine. Instead, it turns suffering into its own kind of joy.

Paul Graham describes the difference quite well in his essay “A Project of Your Own.”

You have moments of happiness when things work out, but they don't last long, because then you're on to the next problem. So why do it at all? Because to the kind of people who like working this way, nothing else feels as right. You feel as if you're an animal in its natural habitat, doing what you were meant to do — not always happy, maybe, but awake and alive.

There is a deep peace in knowing who you are and what your day will hold, in never having to decide what to do and what matters. You do not wander. You are never lost. You are always home.

Hayao Miyazaki, the genius behind movies like Spirited Away, Princess Mononoke, and The Wind Rises is the same. In fact, his need to practice his craft is so compelling that, even after sixty years, he leaves no room for younger talent to try their wings and eventually succeed him. His dedication is both the making and the downfall of Studio Ghibli.

Miyazaki’s The Wind Rises is, on the surface, an inspirational story of another Jirō: Jirō Horikoshi, designer of the Zero fighter plane used in World War II. The real subject is obsession matching Miyazaki’s own: how it animates, isolates, and blissfully consumes you.

Jirō isn’t patriotic, political, or particularly heroic. He just loves airplanes. He loves them the way artists love beauty: helplessly and selfishly. He pours every ounce of himself into designing the perfect aircraft even as his world falls apart. The planes are beautiful but doomed: created for flight, drafted into war.

Miyazaki has said repeatedly that The Wind Rises was his most personal film. To him, his movies are like Jirō’s planes. Beautiful tributes to nature, to nonviolence, to the simple wonder of childhood, to the Japan he grew up in, all things the world has no care for preserving.

Like Jirō, he sacrificed ease, relationships, even his health in pursuit of excellence. He reportedly chain-smoked his way through production, drawing every frame by hand, alienating his team with impossible standards, going back on his promise to step back and retire because he couldn’t stay away. It’s a confession as much as it is a tribute.

Not everyone is obsessive by nature. But I honestly believe obsession can be cultivated.

So how do you find your obsession?

Passion is a feeling; obsession is a practice. Like any practice, it gets sharper the longer you stay with it.

As I write this I’m the realizing that the transformation of someone who is distractible or anhedonic into someone who is obsessed with their craft merits its own essay. Going into in depth here would make this ridiculously long. So I’m going to outline the broad sketches, and my next piece on observing happiness will be a more practical how-to.

  • be determined, not obstinate

  • work on it as a project of your own, not just within the confines of school and work

  • ideally pick a thing you have at least some curiosity about

  • ideally pick a thing you where you have some comparative advantage

  • learn to see progress itself as success, not external recognition or monetizable results.

  • learn to be consistent and don’t get impatient.

  • surround yourself with other people who love work for its own sake

It’s fulfilling because it is simple but not easy. You don’t have to ask yourself what to do next; the work tells you. Most unrelated things, many of which would have once bothered you, fade quietly into the background. You simply have to pursue the thread that tugs you incessantly and build a life that gives it room to breathe.

Obsession teaches you to find motivation in imperfection, creativity in constraints, and meaning in work for its own sake. Sometimes it can take you to greatness, but it always creates contentment.

I’ve often talked about my life paradoxically became more and less stressful since I started making work the center of it. I’m always thinking and talking about all the work I have to do, but I look forward to working, and, more importantly, I don’t indulge anything that complicates my life.

I used to permit (and in permitting, encourage) drama and needless worry. This was because there was no opportunity cost to being imposed on by petty concerns or people.

Now I have no patience for it, simply because it interrupts my work. As a result, my boundaries are much firmer, my life is much more peaceful, and I recognize in myself a burgeoning similarity to S: ridiculously contented, supremely unbothered, unfailingly pleasant, and selfish with my time.

People sometimes envy the simplicity of my life. I wish I could tell them their apparently all-consuming problems would vanish the minute they found a sense of purpose.

No one is obsessed when they’re just starting out.

The lower of the skill curve is clumsy, frustrating, and disenchanting. But as you keep going, your workflow starts to smooth out. You develop taste. You start to notice small problems other people don’t see. You start to fix them.

You also have to be extremely stubborn, virtually impossible to discourage in fact. The path to massaging the octopus is long and painful and full of false turns. But you have to be stubborn in the right way: relentless about showing up, yes, but also relentlessly eager to discover ways you are wrong or could do things better. This naturally arrives when your end goal is getting better and not just becoming the best relative to those around you. A 10% improvement should feel the same whether you’re just starting out or you’re already at the top.

“The persistent are attached to the goal. The obstinate are attached to their ideas about how to reach it.”

from “Persistence” by Paul Graham

I’m reminded of Hokusai’s lament as he lay dying: “If heaven had only granted me five more years, I may at last have become a great painter.”

This from a man who may have been the greatest masters of ukiyo-e ever. Hokusai worked in ink, where one bad mark could ruin a piece that took months. So he learned to move his brush like water — intuitively, gracefully, every stroke communicating powerful vitality. Greatness, to him, was never really a destination. It was something always just out of reach, an ideal glimpsed but never grasped. The anima of his life was simply the journey towards it, like Reepicheep’s quest for Utter East:

“While I can, I sail east in the Dawn Treader. When she fails me, I paddle east in my coracle. When she sinks, I shall swim east with my four paws. And when I can swim no longer, if I have not reached Aslan’s country, or shot over the edge of the world in some vast cataract, I shall sink with my nose to the sunrise.”

C.S. Lewis, Voyage of the Dawn Treader

That’s the spirit I recognize in both Jirōs, in Miyazaki, in S and in the other friends I admire most. It’s not passion, though it seems like it. It’s not confidence, though it seems like that too. It’s more like a sort of humble relentlessness, a hunger that endlessly refines itself until it’s as keen and subtle as a knife.

If you’re not obsessed with anything, it’s because you’ve never managed to pick a thing and keep at it for long enough. The enemy of obsession is impatience. This way of life grows quietly out of consistency, competence, and curiosity. It’s a virtuous cycle: when work is your purpose, getting good is inevitable. You get good enough at anything, the sense of purpose will find you.
